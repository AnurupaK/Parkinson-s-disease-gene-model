Parkinson's Gene based modelling
Project Overview

Parkinson's disease (PD) is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder with complex genetic underpinnings. In this project, we leverage gene expression data from the Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO), specifically dataset GSE54536, which comprises whole-transcriptome profiles of peripheral blood from untreated stage 1 PD patients and healthy controls. Through differential gene expression analysis, functional enrichment, and gene interaction network construction, this research identifies key genes and pathways involved in PD pathogenesis, particularly focusing on endocytosis and alternative splicing mechanisms. The findings provide valuable insights into the molecular mechanisms of early-stage PD, offering potential targets for diagnostic and therapeutic strategies.
Project Outline

About Parkinson

Parkinson's disease (PD) is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder that primarily affects movement, causing symptoms such as tremors, stiffness, and bradykinesia (slowness of movement). It is the second most common neurodegenerative disorder after Alzheimer's disease and primarily affects individuals over the age of 60. The exact cause of PD remains unclear, but it is thought to result from a combination of genetic and environmental factors. At the cellular level, the disease is characterized by the loss of dopamine-producing neurons in the brain, particularly in the substantia nigra, and the presence of Lewy bodiesâ€”abnormal aggregates of the protein alpha-synuclein. In addition to motor symptoms, PD often leads to cognitive and emotional changes, impacting a patient's overall quality of life. Understanding the genetic mechanisms underlying PD is critical for developing effective treatments and early diagnostic tools.

Making the dataset

The dataset used for this project is derived from the Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO) and consists of the supplementary file named GSE54536_Non-normalized_data.txt.gz, which contains two key files. The first file, GPL10558_HumanHT-12_V4_0_R1_15002873_B, provides essential information about the genes, including Source, Gene names, Entrez IDs, Gene Ontology (GO) terms, and Probe IDs. The second file, GSE54536_series_matrix.txt, contains gene expression values associated with these Probe IDs. To create the final dataset for analysis, these two files were merged based on the Probe IDs, resulting in a comprehensive dataset for Parkinson's disease (PD). From this final dataset, key columns such as ILMN_Gene, Symbol, and the gene expression values for all samples were extracted for further analysis, allowing for a focused investigation into the gene expression profiles associated with PD.

Data Analysis

  • Gene Expression Visualization: Various plots were generated to visualize gene expression profiles in Parkinson's disease (PD) patients compared to healthy controls, highlighting upregulated and downregulated genes.
  • ANOVA Analysis: An Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) was conducted to assess the significance of gene expression differences, identifying genes with statistically significant variations between PD and non-PD patients.
  • Gene Ontology (GO) Enrichment Analysis: GO enrichment analysis was performed to determine the biological processes and pathways that are overrepresented among differentially expressed genes, providing insights into the underlying mechanisms of PD.
  • Gene Interaction Network: A gene interaction network was constructed to explore the relationships between the identified genes, enhancing the understanding of molecular interactions contributing to the pathogenesis of Parkinson's disease.
  • Overall Insights: This multifaceted analysis enhances the understanding of the gene expression landscape in PD and highlights potential targets for further research and therapeutic intervention.
Note Section

In the dataset, there are a total of 47,231 genes; however, due to redundancy, it is important to create a subset of the data where genes are grouped, and their expression levels are summed. This approach allows for the calculation of the mean expression levels for both Parkinson's disease (PD) and non-PD samples. The following code demonstrates how to perform this procedure: 
    Group the dataset by gene identifier and sum the expression levels
    df_modify = df.groupby('ILMN_Gene', as_index=False).sum()

    Calculate the mean expression levels for PD samples
    df_modify['Mean_PD'] = df_modify[['GSM1318547', 'GSM1318548', 'GSM1318549', 'GSM1318550',
    'GSM1318551']].mean(axis=1)

    Calculate the mean expression levels for non-PD samples
    df_modify['MeanNon_PD'] = df_modify[['GSM1318552', 'GSM1318553', 'GSM1318554', 'GSM1318555',
    'GSM1318556']].mean(axis=1)
This code will create a modified DataFrame that includes the summed expression levels of genes and the calculated mean expression levels for both PD and non-PD samples.

MA Plot

  • X-axis (A): Represents the average expression level of genes, allowing for a direct comparison of gene activity across samples.
  • Y-axis (M): Indicates the log2 fold change in expression levels, which helps identify genes that are significantly upregulated or downregulated.
  • Colors and Markers:
    • Red dots: Represent upregulated genes, indicating a significant increase in expression levels associated with the condition under study.
    • Blue dots: Indicate downregulated genes, showing a notable decrease in expression that may relate to disease mechanisms.
    • Gray dots: Highlight genes with no significant change in expression, serving as a reference for unaffected genes in the dataset.
  • Interpretation:
    • Points above the horizontal line (y=0) signify upregulated genes (red), while points below this line indicate downregulated genes (blue).
    • The spread of points illustrates the variability in gene expression changes across the dataset, with a wider spread suggesting more pronounced differences.
    • A central cluster near the horizontal line represents genes with little to no change in expression, indicating a baseline level of activity that remains stable.
    • This plot is instrumental in pinpointing which genes are significantly affected in the context of Parkinson's Disease, providing insights into potential biological pathways and mechanisms at play.

Upregulated Genes Analysis

  • After performing the Benjamini-Hochberg test, several key upregulated genes were identified in Parkinson's Disease (PD):
    • OSBPL10: Indicates heightened immune responses.
    • HLA-DQA1: Suggests involvement in inflammatory processes.
    • TLR10: Reflects alterations in lipid metabolism.
    • CD24: Associated with changes in cell signaling and communication.
    • CXCR4: May contribute to neuronal survival and adaptation mechanisms.
  • These upregulated genes suggest an inflammatory environment and cellular adaptations in response to stress or damage in PD.

Downregulated Genes Analysis

  • The Benjamini-Hochberg test revealed significant downregulation of genes in PD:
    • LOC92017: Implicates reduced neuronal plasticity.
    • FOS: Suggests compromised cellular functions critical for neuronal health.
    • PKD1: Reflects a loss of protective mechanisms within neurons.
    • RRP12, LOC642393, NPCDR1, KIAA1530, and JSRP1: Additional genes indicating similar trends in reduced expression.
  • This downregulation points to impairments in the ability of neurons to cope with stressors associated with PD, emphasizing potential targets for therapeutic intervention.

GO Enrichment Analysis Bar Chart Interpretation

  • X-axis: Represents the counts of genes associated with specific Gene Ontology (GO) terms, illustrating the prevalence of genes linked to particular biological functions.
  • Y-axis: Displays various GO terms categorized into three types:
    • Orange bars: Represent Biological Process (BP) terms, such as "regulation of cellular processes" and "protein binding." These processes are crucial in PD as they can affect neuronal health and communication, influencing disease progression.
    • Blue bars: Indicate Cellular Component (CC) terms, including "cytoplasm" and "membrane." Changes in cellular components can affect cell signaling and interactions, playing a vital role in neuronal function and survival.
    • Green bars: Reflect Molecular Function (MF) terms, like "small molecule binding" and "ion binding." These functions are significant in PD since they can impact neurotransmitter dynamics and cellular homeostasis, ultimately affecting motor control and cognitive functions.
  • Bar Length: Each bar's length represents the number of genes associated with that particular GO term, with black dots indicating the exact counts. Longer bars suggest a higher number of genes involved in specific biological activities related to PD.
  • Insights: The enriched GO terms highlight key areas of genetic activity relevant to Parkinson's Disease. By focusing on processes, components, and functions that are significantly represented, this analysis provides a clearer picture of the biological mechanisms potentially underlying PD pathology and identifies pathways that could be targeted for therapeutic interventions.
